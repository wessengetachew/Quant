Abstract This research formalizes a deterministic framework for the analysis of coprime k-tuples within finite integer lattices. While asymptotic density is traditionally defined as \(1/\zeta(k)\), finite constraints introduce a geometric residue, denoted as \(\Delta(R)\). We introduce the G-Sector Density Identity to map local population variances and define the Unity Bound (\(\sqrt{3}/\pi\)) as the critical threshold for rational resolution. Experimental data from the GCD Discovery Engine confirms that \(\Delta(R)\) is not stochastic noise but a result of boundary shell truncation of Möbius inversion cycles.

1. Introduction

The study of coprime distribution has historically relied on asymptotic limits. However, computational models require a precise understanding of finite search radii \(R\). This paper bridges the gap between the infinite limit and discrete lattice observation by defining specific sectors of the Farey sequence and their corresponding density identities.

2. The Sector Density Identity

In a Farey sequence of order \(N\), the population of coprime pairs within a specific sector \(n\) is governed by the inverse square of the index. We define the density \(C(n, N)\) as:

$$C(n, N) = \frac{3N^2}{\pi^2 n(n+1)}$$

2.1 The Unity Bound (The G-Constant)

The "Unity Bound" represents the transition where the density of a sector becomes exactly 1. This identifies the limit of rational resolution within the lattice. Solving for \(n\) when \(C(n, N) = 1\) provides the scaling constant:

$$n_{unity} \approx N \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\pi} \approx 0.55132 \cdot N$$

This constant implies an underlying 2D geometric structure—specifically hexagonal circle packing—inherent in the distribution of rational numbers.

3. The Boundary Cancellation Principle (BCP)

The error term \(\Delta(R)\) is the residual difference between the exact count of coprime k-tuples \(N(R)\) and the expected asymptotic value:

$$\Delta(R) = \sum_{d=1}^{R} \mu(d) \lfloor \frac{R}{d} \rfloor^k - \frac{R^k}{\zeta(k)}$$

The Boundary Cancellation Principle asserts that \(\Delta(R)\) originates at the truncation limits of the lattice. Because the search radius is finite, the Möbius inversion cycle remains incomplete at the boundary shell, leading to a deterministic residue. Experimental analysis shows that this residue is structurally concentrated at the truncation limits, rather than being randomly distributed.

4. Identity Growth and Decomposition

To analyze the "metabolism" of the lattice, we decompose the population into four identities based on coordinate primality (Prime: P, Composite: C):

Identity Type Growth Characteristics Description
PP (Prime-Prime) Logarithmic Growth Strictly bounded by the Prime Number Theorem. High information density.
PC / CP Linear-Logarithmic Hybrid identities representing prime-to-composite transitions.
CC (Composite-Composite) Quadratic Growth High-entropy distribution; becomes dominant as the range \(N\) increases.

The observed Identity Decay proves that as \(N \to \infty\), the ratio of Prime-Prime identities to the total population approaches zero, while Composite-Composite identities reach near-total saturation of the lattice growth.

5. Stability Offset and Phase Shift

The mathematical distance between the lattice midpoint and the Unity Bound is defined by the Stability Offset (\(\delta\)):

$$\delta = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\pi} - \frac{1}{2} \approx 0.0513$$

This constant determines the phase shift of the Möbius wave relative to the lattice boundaries. It marks the region where the boundary residue reaches peak oscillation amplitude before undergoing self-correction.
